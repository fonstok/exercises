A Few Terms

Don't skip the terms!

Server

Servers simply put, are computers. They have hard drives, files, folders, ram, processors just like the computers you use. They are specially built/meant for "serving" content like websites, streaming movies, and data. Unlike your computer, they are on 24/7/365 and have a dedicated and specific address so we, the users of the internet, can find and access the content stored on them. FYI, you can make any computer into a server.

So, there should be no mystery here. You are connecting to a computer, and copying files to it, just like a plugging in a portable USB drive and copying files from it to your desktop.

FTP

FTP is an acronym for File Transfer Protocol. If you take a sec. and analyse the words, it's purpose should make some sense. Essentially, computer scientists long ago wrote the rules and process, aka the protocol, for files to be transferred over the internet.

A pet peeve of many in the cool club is to refer to an FTP software's name, as the process of FTP. For instance, "I could not CyberDuck my files to the server!" CyberDuck is an FTP software, and the pet peeve is more than just me being a nit-picky grub slugger. I'm a teacher, and I need to know that you know that you could transfer your files a 1000 different ways and associating the process with one piece of software implies that your blinders may be on and you don't know that and likely will not progress to newer better ways of transferring your files. There are many paid and free application/plugins that enable FTP so keep that in mind or you'll risk peeving the cool club's member's pets.

Cache

Cache (pronounced cash) is a technical process utilized by internet browsers (Firefox, Chrome, Edge) to temporarily save files on users computers after they download them for viewing from a server. They do this to speed up browsing by decreasing the amount of data they have to download. For example, you likely access the Amazon, Twitter, Canvas, or Maryville websites' up to 20 times a day. It's also unlikely that many elements like the logo, interface, etc. that make up the sites will change very often. So, to save time and effort, browsers store image and other files on a folder on your computer so it doesn't needlessly have to download those files 20 times a day.

I'm telling you about cache because a key issue when updating a file is seeing the old "cashed" version of the file you uploaded previously instead of the new file you just uploaded - thus driving you batty. You will be taught how to turn the cache off or load without the cached files.
